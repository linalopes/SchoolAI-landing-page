01

What this
micro-lab is.

“Turn a sheet of paper into an expressive interface
using simple electronics, fast thinking, and public experimentation.”
— Arduino Day 2026 · School of Tomorrow’s AI

This session is built as a live design sprint: not a polished classroom demo, but an open making process. The core challenge is simple and strong — to create, in four hours, an expressive paper interface using low-cost electronics.

The goal is not only to make something that works, but to think through paper as a material for interaction: paper that sings, paper that lights up as feedback, paper that becomes part of a collective instrument, or paper that behaves like a poetic controller.

It speaks directly to a pedagogy of dirty prototypes, fast iteration, and public debugging. The process matters as much as the object.

02

The core
challenge.

Create, in four hours, an expressive paper interface with simple electronics.

Prototype in public

The event is structured around a live build. Rather than presenting a finished object, Lina will think aloud, test fast, improvise, and show how decisions emerge through hands-on making. The audience can follow the logic, the mistakes, the corrections, and the transformation of concept into circuit.

Possible directions

Depending on the energy of the session, the prototype can branch into one or more expressive variations:

  • Paper that sings — paper becomes a sonic trigger, voice surface, or tiny instrument.
  • Paper that lights up — LEDs act as feedback, signal, rhythm, or visual emotion.
  • Paper for collective interaction — multiple hands, contacts, or bodies activate the same circuit or interface.
03

Ideal structure
for 4 hours.

A compact format that combines context, demo, making, debugging, and reflection.

Block 1 · 20 min

Opening & framing

Welcome, context, materials on the table, challenge framing, and a short introduction to the spirit of the session.

Block 2 · 40 min

Three micro demos

Very short demonstrations: conductive paper thinking, LED or buzzer feedback, and one simple input strategy.

Block 3 · 90 min

Main live build

The central prototype is built live, step by step, with room for improvisation and adaptation.

Block 4 · 30 min

Public debugging

Error is treated as method: what failed, what is unstable, and how debugging becomes visible and pedagogical.

Block 5 · 30 min

Aesthetic or conceptual variation

The prototype is pushed beyond function and into expression, form, gesture, or collective interaction.

Block 6 · 20 min

Closing reflections

What worked, what failed, what deserves a second iteration, and where this kind of material research can go next.
